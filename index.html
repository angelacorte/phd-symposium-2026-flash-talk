Advances in Collective Robotics Through Macro-Programming

angela.cortecchia@unibo.it

Challenge

Collective Adaptive Systems are ensembles of sensors, robots, and services that cooperate while devices move, fail, and join at runtime.

State of the art: Aggregate Computing offers reusable patterns for collective behavior, but most systems still run as isolated aggregate programs.

Open gap: there is no runtime layer for managing multiple collective processes as first-class entities.

  • processes overlap in space and time, but lack lifecycle and ownership;
  • roles, permissions, signals, and resource flows are application-specific;
  • monitoring, adaptation, and safety are not yet unified as runtime services;
  • failures, mobility, and topology changes require managed recovery.

A Collective Operating System is the missing middleware for running many aggregate processes on shared, heterogeneous devices, with lifecycle, communication, permissions, and resources managed collectively.

Building Blocks

Each contribution implements one capability that a Collective OS must expose as a reusable runtime service.

Programming Collektive

Executable aggregate programs across heterogeneous devices: the programming substrate for collective services.

Coordination Self-stabilizing gossip

Fresh collective state without global resets: obsolete values are pruned after failures and topology changes.

Morphogenesis FieldVMC

Adaptive resource flows and dynamic hierarchies: a first model of collective resource management.

Monitoring Field-Based Distributed Particle Filtering

Distributed monitoring service: observers fuse partial, noisy information into runtime estimates.

Adaptation Runtime replanning

Runtime adaptation service: robot teams repair missions after failures, new tasks, or changing conditions.

Safety Control Lyapunov / Barrier Functions

Safety contract layer: local control constraints keep collective adaptation within safe boundaries.

Research Direction

The contributions in the middle column are not isolated results: they expose services that should be orchestrated by a common collective runtime.

  • Process abstraction: define collective processes with scope, lifecycle, ownership, and interaction rules.
  • Runtime substrate: reuse programming, coordination, monitoring, adaptation, and safety as system services.
  • Governance model: decide who can start, stop, signal, compose, or constrain collective processes.
  • Validation path: stress the runtime on robot teams and smart environments under failures and mobility.

Runtime Questions

When several collective processes share the same devices, the runtime must govern membership, resources, signals, and safety constraints.

who owns it? who joins? who signals? who gets resources?

Takeaway

The thesis goal is a Collective Operating System: a runtime that turns field-based algorithms into deployable, observable, governable, and safe collective applications.
